Request 1:Narrative Project Charter Statement
Coi Cafe Central Flagship Expansion Project 2026 kicks off with a clear target. Opening shop in Da Nang, Hue, Quang Nam, Quang Ngai, and Binh Dinh marks the next move. Five new locations rise up across central regions during one full year. Tourism there keeps climbing, bringing foot traffic along for the ride. Young crowds make up much of the area's residents now. That shift lines up well with where the brand wants to go. Instead of waiting around, reusable cup systems roll out from day one. Beans arrive straight from farms in Dak Lak - no middlemen involved. Cutting transport steps slashes cost while helping the planet too. Each store becomes a hub for local hiring, not just serving drinks but shaping careers. Training programs start before doors even open. Visibility climbs when people see familiar spaces popping up nearby. Growth numbers aim between thirty and forty percent after a year and a half passes. Right now, twelve outlets operate under the same roof. These additions push that base further without changing what made it work. Sustainability stays rooted in daily choices rather than slogans posted on walls. Behind every pour lies a connection - to land, labor, culture. Expansion doesn’t mean losing touch. It means carrying more weight with steady hands.
Exactly five flagship stores will open. Each store measures from one hundred fifty to two hundred fifty square meters. Their interiors follow a uniform look - nostalgic, yet built with sustainable materials. Inside, customers find the usual Coi Cafe offerings alongside a few local drink specialties. Expansion beyond these locations isn’t permitted. Smaller spots won’t happen. Altering how the business operates falls outside boundaries too. Work must finish completely by the twelfth month after launch. Every door opens before Tet 2027 arrives. Eight point five billion Vietnamese dong makes up the full approved spending limit. Holding tight to Coi Cafe’s expectations matters - right down to what goes in each drink, how the space feels, plus how guests are treated. Guest feedback needs to land no lower than four and a half stars from every five. A recognized green business credential must be secured along the way.

Request 2:Main Cost Budget Items Twenty Percent
Five big costs add up to VND 8.5 billion for the project. Starting off, renting good spots and redoing insides the way the brand wants takes about VND 3.2 billion - that is around 640 million per shop across five places. Site checks happened first, then three trusted builders in Da Nang and Hue sent pricing; oversight falls to the Project Manager alongside the design team handling interiors. Next comes gear and furnishings: heavy-duty espresso units, cooling units, seating, old-style decorations, along with cups meant for reuse, tagged at VND 1.8 billion. Figures come from quotes provided by suppliers like Jura and WMF, alongside local furniture sellers - oversight falls to the Operations Manager. Coffee beans sourced from Dak Lak make up part of raw materials, together with regional supplies, packaging, plus reusable cups meant to last half a year; that portion totals around VND 1.1 billion when matching forecasts of three hundred to five hundred drinks sold each day per outlet - the Procurement Manager handles this area. Marketing efforts kick off with online ads, team-ups with influencers active in Central Vietnam, launch parties, also sessions to brief employees - all set at roughly VND 900 million thanks to insights drawn from past initiatives and outside firm estimates - with guidance led by the Marketing Director. Hiring baristas, instructing new supervisors, setting up checkout software, paying early wages for about three months add up to nearly VND 1.5 billion considering going pay levels for teams ranging between fifteen and twenty people per location - that work sits under direction of the HR and Training Team. Somewhere between five and seven percent tucked away as backup funds fits into the overall financial plan.

Request 3:Three Project Risks
A few major risks stand out in the project plan. Heavy rains or typhoons in Central Vietnam might push back construction timelines - permits moving slowly through offices create similar problems. Such delays often stretch schedules by two or even three months. Costs may climb between VND 300 and 500 million when workers sit idle or fines pile up. Rushing tasks near the end could also weaken overall build quality. One way to handle problems is picking builders who know what they do, kicking off building in summer of 2026 - June or July - and having extra spots ready just in case. When delays stretch past a month, the move is shifting to triple shifts on site. Rising material expenses form the next concern, tied to swings in coffee rates or broader price hikes across the economy. Costs might climb between fifteen and twenty percent, around two hundred to three hundred million Vietnamese dong. Quality may dip if cheaper materials get swapped in - or prices go up at sale points. Project range shrinks too, cutting back unique regional goods. Signing long-term fixed-price deals with growers helps reduce risk, although spreading purchases across several suppliers adds another layer of backup. When it comes to low customer turnout at launch, hesitation might come from people not knowing the brand or crowded markets getting in the way. That kind of situation risks income drops, harm to how the name is seen, delays reaching profit balance, along with added spending on ads just to catch up. To stay ahead, checking what buyers want happens earlier - about eight weeks out - with team-ups involving well-known locals who influence choices and small sampling gatherings held around town. If things stall anyway, fallback steps involve short bursts of heavy promotion like get one free when buying one during opening days.

Request 4:RACI Chart Overview and Responsibilities
Someone has to lead, so the Project Manager takes charge of nearly everything here. When it comes to planning, that person owns the outcome even though the Market Development Director gets asked for input - everyone else just hears about it later. Picking a location and working out lease details? The Project Manager decides, but the Market Development Director handles execution. Designing interiors and managing renovations lands on the Project Manager’s shoulders, yet the Interior Team does the actual work. Someone always watches procurement - the Project Manager guides it, though the Procurement team does the work. Marketing campaigns move forward under the Project Manager’s direction, yet the Marketing team holds the load. Bringing in staff and teaching them falls to the Project Manager, but HR plus Training make it happen. The big launch and passing things over rest with the Project Manager, even if the Market Development Director answers for results - and everyone else gets asked along the way.
Ten main jobs make up the work, each with who handles what. Starting off, picking where to set up comes after checking demand - that task belongs to the Project Manager, though the Market Development Director also carries weight there. Next follows deal talks on renting space: the manager owns it, but Legal along with Market Development do the heavy lifting. Then comes signing off how spaces look inside - again the lead rests with the manager while design choices fall to the Interior crew. After that, getting approval for changes needs paperwork handled by the same interior group under oversight from the top organizer. Equipment and furniture buying answers to the Project Manager while working under the Procurement team. Staff hiring follows direction from the Project Manager but runs through HR. The barista training program depends on the Project Manager yet gets handled by the Training group. Creating launch promotions ties back to the Project Manager, though led by Marketing. Testing during soft opening rests with the Project Manager, using input from every team involved. Later on, the Project Manager handles the Grand Opening Event along with handing over the store, while the Market Development Director answers for it. Following that, a review after launch might happen - this one also stays with the Project Manager..
